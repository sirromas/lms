Controversial Tax Bill Nears Passage
～   Week of February 5, 2018   ～

"I really believe the economic growth that will come from this law will dramatically help reduce any deficit effects that come from it," said Republican Speaker of the House, Paul Ryan.
The Republican-controlled Senate has joined the GOP-led House of Representatives in passing the largest tax overhaul in thirty years. Its passage involved a stunningly quick trip through Congress for such complex legislation.

Presiding over the Senate, Vice President Mike Pence announced the 51-49 vote to applause from Republicans. Sen. Bob Corker, R-Tenn., was the only lawmaker to cross party lines, joining the Democrats in opposition.

Democratic Senators derided the accelerated process, criticizing their Republican colleagues for hatching the bill in secret and then crafting it so hastily that lawmakers were handed a rough version that still had scribbles-in-the-margin. There was little time to review and discuss before having to vote later that night, at 2 a.m.

"The bill was given to us on a few hours notice," Democratic Senate Minority Leader Charles Schumer complained, "and has not been read fully or considered fully by a single senator." Adding a congressional excalaimation point to his objections, Schumer made a doomed attempt to block the bill: "I move we adjourn," he pleaded prior to the vote, "so we can first read and then clean up this awful piece of legislation.” n

"This tax cut really is not for the middle class,it’s for the rich,” Sen. Brown accused. "I’ve been here working my whole stinkin’ career for people who don’t have a chance," Sen. Hatch countered. "And I really resent anybody saying that I’m just doing this for the rich."

This legislation is "removed from the reality of what the American people need," Schumer charged. Excoriating Republicans for releasing a 479-page bill that no one could fully absorb, Schumer insisted that because of their actions, "the Senate is descending to a new low of chicanery."

Republican Senator Ron Johnson defended the process. "You really don't read this kind of legislation," he said, explaining that senators focused on major provisions in the tax reform bill and not the "mind numbing" comparisons to current law.

Trump's inability to achieve three of his first-year legiasltiave priorities—repeal President Obama's "Affordable Healthcare Act," hasten the closure of Special Consul Robert Mueller's investigation into Russia's interference in the 2916 U.S. presidential election, and get Mexico to pay for the construction of a security wall across the entire Mexican-American border—have underminded confidence in his leadership.

A recent Quinnipiac University Poll revealed a 44% positive rating for President Trump's navigation of the economy, yet his overall job approval is stuck at historic lows, at just 35%.

A 62% majority of Americans give the economy favorable marks and yet another Quinniac survey showed that by a 50%-37% margin, most people believe that Barack Obama deserves credit for the current economic recovery, not Donald Trump. Touted as an astute businessman and dealmaker who can dramatically boost the wages of the average working man and woman, Republican lawmakers are convinced that if these negative assessments of President Trump's first year in office are going to dissipate, a major economic initiative like passing tax cuts is needed.

GOP leaders are determined to pass the measure rapidly before opposition Democrats and lobbying groups can generate enough public outcry to blow it up. Passage of the bill is crucial to shifting the political narrative among Republicans from one of legislative failure to a fresh start that features the monumental shake-up of the tax code that conservatives have been pushing for decades. Most congressional Republicans believe the bill's passage is essential to retaining their House and Senate majorities in next year's elections.

The bipartisan United States Congress Joint Committe on Taxation panel found that for many families, tax savings from the bill would be modest and by 2027, those earning under $75,000 would on average face higher, not lower, taxes.

Republicans disagree, stating that the tax package would benefit people of all incomes. Democrats vigorously denounce it as a giveaway to the rich. Not a single Democrat from either chamber voted for the bill.

In making the case for the charge that Republicans employed a formula whereby tax cuts for the wealthy are paid for by raising taxes on the middle class, the bill's opponents cite such provisions as a reduction in the Federal government's corporate rate from 35% to 21% and the imposition of a new tax on graduate students who receive tution waivers in exchange for working as research assistants. They will now have to pay taxes on their financial assistance, support which barely covers livng expenses.

Many students could find themselves with a $5,000 tax bill for payment on the dollar equivlalent of whatever tuition, meals, housing, and/or books granted to them by the unversity. This exchange—tax breaks for corporations and tax hikes for students—is precisely the "Robin Hood in reverse" equation that has earned the ire of so many Democrats.
Republicans are vigorously pushing back against the "tax cuts for the rich, tax hikes for the middle class" epithet. Lindsey Burke, Director of the Center for Education Policy at the conservative Heritage Foundation, objects to the notion that the tax bill threatens higher education.

Although it eliminates deductions for tution waivers and interest on student loans, Burke notes, the legislation "leaves intact another provision of the tax code—section 117(a)—which provides that scholarships used to pay college tuition are not considered taxable income." Although she defends taxing compensation for graduate teaching assistants as "a move also consistent with the broader aim of tax reform," Burke suggests a workaround to avoid paying the tax.

"Universities can simply re-classify tuition waivers (which are often provided to graduate students in exchange for serving as teaching assistants, and would be considered taxable income) as scholarships, which section 117(a) would still consider non-taxable income," Burke advises. "Universities would likely simply restructure their aid packages to continue to accommodate graduate students," she predicts. "So scholarships, which are not considered income, would remain untaxed."

Aside from liberal attacks on the Republican bill as a tax cut for the rich, prospects for passage hit a snag after the Committee on Taxation determined that the legislation would worsen federal shortfalls by $1 trillion over a decade, even when factoring in the economic growth that supporters say would result from the reduction in taxes. GOP leaders scrambled to defend the legislation from conservative Republican critics opposed to such a large surge in the federal deficit. They insisted that the tax cuts would spur so much economic growth that they would pay for themselves.

To offset revenue losses from the cuts, the economy would have to grow 3% annually, thereby producing more wealth and hence more wages and profts to tax. "Increase the gross domestic product growth by 1 percentage point," notes Commerce Secretary Wilbur Ross, and over the next decade the "$10 trillion more GDP" will yield "$3 trillion more revenues to the federal government."

Although there has been periodic GDP growth of nearly 3% in 2017, most economists do not believe that such a robust clip is sustainable. The last time the U.S. had an annual GDP growth rate of 3% was twelve years ago.

In addition to the two official government bodies scoring the bill, the Congressional Budget Office and the Joint Committee on Taxation, additional studies by the Tax Policy Center, Goldman Sachs, and the Wharton School of Businesses also conclude that the stimulus derived from the tax cut won't be enough to propel the economy into an average 3% annual GDP growth rate across the enteriety of the next decade. Without the extra revenue generated from a more rapidly expanding economy, the shortfall will force the government into debt.

Still, there are Republican lawmakers who dispute these projections. “I am absolutely convinced that this bill will end up reducing the deficit,” Sen. Rob Portman (R-OH) told reporters. “I feel very good about the fiscal situation.”

The conservative Tax Foundation supports this view. "Our model results indicate that the plan would be pro-growth," ther analysis states. "Wages, long stagnant, would increase by 2.9 percent, while the reform would produce 925,000 new jobs."

Treasury Secretary Steven Mnuchin used the GDP estimate when evaluating the bill's impact. “What we’ve scored it to is 2.9 percent GDP over 10 years, which is scaling up to 3 percent,” Mnuchin said at The Atlantic and Aspen Institute’s Washington Ideas forum. “We think that’s very, very doable; we think we can do higher than that, but if we get to the 3 percent, that’s $2 trillion in additional revenue.”

A central pillar of the Republican approach to economic growth is the belief that high taxes are preventing American businesses from being competitive in global markets, citing average U.S. rates on corporate income of 38.9% as being the highest among the developed nations in the 35-member Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development. Democrats are quick to note that there are average statutory rates like the 38.9% mandated by law in the United States, and the 18.6% effective one which is actually paid.

"In order to give tax breaks to billionaires and to large, profitable corporation, they're going to cut programs for the elderly, the children, working families of this country, and the poor," Sen. Sanders protested. "This legislation will go down in history as one of the worst, most unfair pieces of legislation ever passed." 

"The statutory rate isn’t the only thing that matters with respect to corporate taxation," reported a Tax Foundation analysis based on data from the nonpartisan Congressional Budget Office. "The U.S. corporate tax and corporate tax codes throughout the world are filled with credits and deductions that impact both corporate investment behavior and how much corporations end up remitting in tax. And just as the statutory rate varies across the world, these deductions and credits vary."

When adjusting for those variations and measuring effective corporate tax rates, comparisons between the U.S. and competing nations change dramatically. For instance, America's statutory taxes average 8.7% above Germany's but its effective rate is only 3.1% higher. The differences become more dramatic when compared to other countries: America's statutory rate is 8.9% higher than Japan's but when using the effective number, the U.S tax is actually 3.1% lower. Compared to the U.K., the official U.S. rate is 19.9% higher yet the effective one is .7% lower.

There are various economic models for computing the array of deductions, government subsidies, tax exemptions, and other credits that generate the effective rate. And there are some dissenters who contradict the findings that U.S. corporations are not overtaxed when compared internationally. The Business Roundtable, the Alliance for Competitive Taxation, and PricewaterhouseCoopers maintain that both the U.S. statutory and effective corporate tax rates rank among the highest in the developed world.

Facing unanimous Democratic opposition with only a slim 52-48 Senate majority, the Republican leadership in the Senate could afford to lose no more than two GOP senators (in the event of a tie, the Vice President casts the tie-breaking vote). Despite the projected budget deficits predicted by most mainstream economists, which have traditionally been an anathema to the conservative "Tea Party" wing of the Republican Party, congressional leaders were able to persaude these holdouts that there was no downside to supporting the bill.

If the establishment Republicans are right, they noted, the tax cuts pay for themselves through the funds paid to the government through new paid by workers newly employed in plants that would not otherwise have been built if corporations did not have the money to invest that's leftover from decreased taxes.

If the establisment Republicans are wrong, then it will be politically easier to make the case for dramatic cutbacks in social spending which has been a cause célèbre for generations of conservatives. Republicans could simply argue that soaring deficits threaten to bankrupt the coounty and programs like Medicare and Social Secrity will have to be cut back and perhaps eventually eliminated because there's not enough money to pay for them. Since the programs are so popular, analysts reason that it's more likely that public opinion will acqueise to their demise if framed as an affordability issue rather than one about taxes and government priorities.

Sen. Corker wasn't buying it. Despite assurances from his fellow Republicans, the projected revenue loss worried the veteran deficit hawk, prompting him into becoming the sole GOP dissenter. "Obviously I'm kind of a dinosaur on the fiscal issues," said Corker, who has long championed the traditionally conservative call for balanced budgets.

Some observers wonder if the Republican success in passing a final bill might be a pyhrric victory give the public opposition to the legislation. They believe the prospect of increasing debts due to a shortfall in revenue from taxes will alienate conservatives while liberals will continue to oppose the legislation's cuts in social programs. Sen. Corker gives voice to the concerns of his fellow Republicans who might be considering splitting from party leaders to block the bill.

"I also have taken some really tough votes against very popular policies, including appropriations bills, budget resolutions, defense authorizations, disaster funding, and even a veterans’ bill," Corker wrote. "This is yet another tough vote. I am disappointed. I wanted to get to yes. But at the end of the day, I am not able to cast aside my fiscal concerns and vote for legislation that I believe, based on the information I currently have, could deepen the debt burden on future generations."

Other GOP members of Congress have concerns rooted more firmly in politcals principle: the bill is extremely unpopular among voters despite their desire for tax reform. A recent Harris Poll reported that although 61% of the respondents favor reform and 64% support lowering taxes, 64% are opposed to the Republican bill with only 36% in favor. A Quinnipiac University survey showed even less support at just 29% and an average of five polls had opposition as high as 46%. More people supported the 1990 George H.W. Bush and 1993 Bill Clinton tax hikes than the 2016 Trump tax reform.

Republican Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell has shrugged off polls finding scant public enthusiasm for the measure that would impact nearly every aspect of Americans society. "Big bills are rarely popular," McConnell acknowledged. Still, Sen. McConnell insists, the bill will eventually prove its worth.

McConnell also felt that the public would reward the GOP for its fidelity to it's campaign pledge to cut corporate taxes and adjust others. "I think the American people wanted to see what having a Republican Congress means and it's certainly a promise made and a promise kept."
